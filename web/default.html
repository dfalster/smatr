Linear models are often used in ecological and evolutionary studies. In particular scaling relationships of the form log Y = log a + B log X have been used extensively in studies of:
1. Plant functional trait associations
2. Life history traits in relation to body size
3. Ecological stoichiometry

In each case, hypotheses primarily concern the slope (B) and intercept (log a) of a relationship rather than its strength. When one is interested in estimating a set of Y values from a set of X values, ordinary linear regression is the most appropriate method to use. If, however, the purpose is to describe the major axis along which two variables are correlated, regression is misleading because it systematically underestimates Y at one end and overestimates at the other, compared to the central axis of a cloud of points. This is because residuals from the "line of best fit" are calculated in the Y-dimension only.

A more appropriate method in these situations is Standardised Major Axis (SMA, or reduced major axis, RMA) analysis. Unlike regression, residuals for the SMA "line of best fit" are calculated in both the X and Y dimensions. Until recently, a major limitation to research in the area has been the lack of statistical algorithms and software that provide for rigorous comparison of SMA lines among groups of data.

(S)MATR is a software program designed for making statistical comparisons of SMA relationships among groups of data. For example, the relationship between two functional traits can be compared among groups of species from different locations. The software makes use of recently derived statistical algorithms to test for a common slope among groups, and test for differences in elevations among groups with a common slope. In addition, (S)MATR can be used to fit SMA relationships to individual groups and compare the slope of these relationships to a hypothesised value.

System requirements: (S)MATR is a simple DOS-based program that will run in any PC Windows environment. There are no minimum memory requirements, however, the program will naturally run faster on faster computers. Computer speed may be an issue if you are working with large datasets.
